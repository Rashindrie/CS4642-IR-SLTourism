It appears as though Liberty Arcade managed to hit the spot the Racecourse somehow missed. The swankier Liberty Plaza offshoot has a fairly interesting bunch of shops and restaurants, notably including Oro 1889 and the Listening Room. The newest addition is the Japanese fusion restaurant Poppy Hana (literal translation: Poppy flower). Its USP is the country's first/only sushi conveyor belt - also termed a sushi-go-round, kaiten zushi, or sushi train. The original Poppy Hana is a popular London restaurant, now open in Sri Lanka (it's not a franchise or anything though). It's just in the soft opening stage as yet, so expect more additions to their menu etc.

Dining Logistics

For those not in the loop, the system can be a little intimidating at first. Basically, there's a series of dishes that shuttle around the restaurant on the conveyor belt. They are each placed on a different coloured plate, which denotes their price. There aren't too many waiters as this is a self-service place, so you need to keep your eye on the belt in case you miss the plate you want.



The pink dishes are Rs. 180, yellow Rs. 250, white Rs. 295, grey Rs. 345, red Rs. 430, and black Rs. 495. At the end of your meal, the waiter will add up the plate prices and hand you your bill. You're also free to order a la carte stuff from the menu, as well as drinks.

Food & Drinks

The variety is admittedly pretty great. They have an open-plan kitchen, so you can see the Japanese chef and his minions slicing up a storm in there. Their range includes the usual sushi options, complemented by tempura, donburi, Japanese curry, and daily specials. I'd say you can expect to pay between Rs. 1000 to Rs. 1500 for a proper meal of about 4 belt dishes including a drink. Apart from the belt and the menu, we were pleasantly surprised to hear they whip up tailor-made dishes according to your specifications too. We tried a special tempura roll, which came in a funky dragon shape an including tempura prawn, avocado, and vinegared rice in a seaweed roll. It was incredibly tasty and came to about Rs. 900 for a plate (pictured below).



We also tried a couple of nigiris, prawn and squid, both of which were somewhat plain but fresh. There are a few pretty solid vegetarian options which we also experimented with - an egg salad, a sort of omelette nigiri, and an avocado and cucumber roll. The egg salad was on a pink dish, at Rs. 180, and quite tasty and worth it as a starter. We also tried a sort of fish patty, and what appeared to be a chinese roll filled with crab which was very oddly sweet. The tempura cuttlefish was a favourite with us, specially with a dab of chillie oil.



The specials included a "lamb chop with attitude" accompanied by roasted vegetables and spicy teriyaki sauce on a black plate, and a few red snapper sushi options. There's only one dessert option for now, a green tea ice cream which we didn't try but sounds like it could be interesting as long as they avoid the two extremes of authentic bitterness and sugary flavouring.

The drinks menu is extensive, ranging from the usual soft drinks and fresh juices (although an orange juice is an inexplicably steep Rs. 425) to an exotic selection of Japanese tea and fruit smoothies. They don't have a liquor licence, but it's BYOB with no corkage fee, so bring a bottle of sake and some friends.

Service & Ambience

The ambience is quite modern, with red panelled walls teamed with blue lights and shiny surfaces. The place maintains a red, white and black colour scheme which makes for bold lines and a fairly chic vibe. It's not fine dining or fancy, and definitely seems like a casual place, but is still well appointed and presented. You can choose between the long bar-like seating against the belt or a few tables for 2 and 4 on the other side. They also have an outdoor area but that doesn't appear to be open as yet.



The service is quite friendly. We got lots of "irasshaimase!" greetings as we entered and "oyasumi nasai!" goodbyes as we left, which was a nice touch (reminiscent of the slightly creepier BreadTalk hollers).

My one issue was that when we asked a waitress how we were supposed to identify what was on the plates going past on the belt (they don't have labels), she said "well we expect you'd already know". It wasn't said rudely or anything, but I genuinely doubt the average Lankan patron can identify every Japanese dish on sight. We ended up just experimenting or asking a waiter each time something looked enticing.

Conclusion

Poppy Hana is a fun experience with good food, and lots of variety. It seems like a great place for a work lunch - quick and fun. I think Colombo has been in dire need of a non-dodgy sushi place at moderate prices and great ambience/quality. However, I question the sustainability of the vast amounts of dishes they have to keep rolling around on the belt in order to provide a decent variety. Colombo's just too small to provide the kind of numbers to justify the quantity of food, and I feel as though a lot probably gets wasted at the end of the day. It may be kaiten but I'm not sure it's kaizen in these circumstances.

TIP

Try their tempura rolls, and be open to experimentation.
