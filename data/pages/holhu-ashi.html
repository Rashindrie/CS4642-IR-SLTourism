Holhu Ashi is now sadly closed down.

Holhu Ashi is our Maldivian food recommendation; a lovely beach- shack/cum cafe restaurant on Dehiwala beach at the end of Vanderwert Place, next to Lani’s Seafood Restaurant. As befits a restaurant from the world's most sought after beach destination it's located just meters from the sea. There's a small cafe-like front and a very spacious and rather romantic rear seating area right on the beach.

Its really an amazingly authentic place with the owners and virtually all the customers being Maldivian. The cryptic squiggles of the Divehi script (it really originated as a secret code) advertising specials and wi-fi adorns the walls. While there are English menus -mas aluvi roshi or rihakuru diya dont make much sense even when written in the roman script so it's best to ask the friendly owner Suhana (or her husband) for recommendations (excellent English and some Tamil is spoken). As we arrived a bit late (well after 9) not everything was available but we were given a selection of Maldivian dishes of the the day (enough for two, less than Rs 800).

Some roshi- basically very light chapatti with a coconut and maldive fish sambol, something like pol sambol but white in color and without chilli. The central dish was a light soup of coconut milk and the uniquely Maldivian rihakuru- a dense paste of boiled down tuna fish that looks like marmite but has a more complex, almost blue cheesy flavor. We also got some crispy sprats, some dry fried curry leaves with a lime and onion relish and a small plate of straight-up rihakuru. While Suhana was keen to stress this was nothing special, just every day food, each dish and condiment had a clear taste of its own and combined- a shred of roshi, filled with coconut and a bit of fish, dunked in the soup, smeared with a little riahakuru and sprinkled with lime - it really was rather good.

What was striking was was how different the flavors were to those found in either Sri Lankan or Indian food. Very sour, and intensely savory without being bitter- sweetened slightly by the grated coconut. Lighter than Sri Lankan food and therefore much lighter than Indian food with a thin coconut milk base and a minimum of spices. There was chilli but not much in the way of discernible curry powder, very minimal cumin, coriander or turmeric which means the flavors of the lime, coconut, fish and chili were very clear.

There was something east asian about the soup but not quite... it's really a different palette of flavors and fully worth trying. The Maldives are our immediate neighbors and while Sri Lankans and Maldivians have rather a lot in common (Sinhalese and Divehi are quite closely related) Sri Lankans tend to know almost nothing about the culture and cuisine of our brothers/sisters from the islands. A visit to Holhu Ashi is very good way to fill some blanks in your cultural/culinary awareness.

We turned up towards the end of the evening so got only what they had on hand but if you order ahead you can get a full maldivian spread for Rs 500 per person. Judging from the everyday meals the feast seems well worth trying and we'll be back to cover more of this very under-rated culinary culture shortly.

On Maldivian Cuisine



Maldivian food may not be one of the world's great cuisines. 101 things to do with dried tuna fish (aka maldive fish) is, or well should be, the gastronomic motto of that unworldly necklace of paradise islands strung across the Indian Ocean to the west of Sri Lanka. It's food based on centuries of hardship and scarcity. Before Americans/Europeans discovered the airplane, and decided that lying on a beach in the blazing sun was a desirable thing to do, this clutch of barren tropical islands, with little fresh water and no possibility of formal agriculture was not an easy place to live in. For the almost exclusively fisheries dependent populace lunch was fish, dinner was fish and breakfast was fish, ideally tuna fish. Manioc and bread-fruit which do grow on the islands were a more regular staple than rice which was an imported luxury. Garudiya- pieces of tuna fish boiled in a lightly salted broth, was traditionally served at almost every meal.

However this overwhelming simplicity doesn’t mean the food of the islands is in anyway untasty or uninteresting. Like any culture confronted with scarcity the hardy people of the Maldives have made maximum use of the few resources at their disposal. Using tuna,coconut flesh, limes, and whatever spices they were able to obtain by trade they created dishes for festivals, feasts and the royal houses of the islands. The result is a rather fascinating and distinctive cuisine which, while borrowing from Sri Lankan and Indian culinary traditions, has a very distinct flavor of its own.
